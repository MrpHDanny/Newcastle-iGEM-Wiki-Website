Project Description

Parkinson’s UK estimates 145,000 people were diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease in the UK in 2018 and that 1 in 37 people will develop PD in their lifetime. Neurological disorders are a leading source of disability and death worldwide [ 1 ]. The burden of neurodegenerative disorders “such as PD” have arisen due to a global increase in population numbers and life expectancy [2, 3] . Current diagnostic procedures rely on the identification of motor symptoms related to PD. By the time motor symptoms are distinguishable, over 60% of all dopamine neurons may have been lost - resulting in significant irreversible neurological damage [ 4 ]. Early diagnoses of PD patients for non-motor symptoms are limited, but can often be misdiagnosed for depression, anxiety, and age-related cognitive decline [ 5 ].

We are attempting to use a triplet biomarker system to provide indication of that an early Parkinson’s diagnosis may be required. By engineering three independent bacteria, E. coli (K12), to detect the presence of specific molecules associated with Parkinson's disease; alpha-synuclein associated transcription factor GATA2, dermal eicosane (characteristic of seborrheic dermatitis - a skin condition associated with suppressed immune systems), and a decrease in the anti-oxidant glutathione. Each biomarker will run independently, and only a collective positive result for all three tests will result in a confirmed need for a diagnosis of Parkinson’s disease. We plan to test experimental controls using pure chemical samples from suppliers, diluted to mimic concentrations found in both patient blood and serum levels.

The Eicosane biosensor

In the study involving Milne, the three main volatile organic compounds – hippuric acid, eicosane and octadecanal – were upregulated in Parkinson’s subjects. Eicosane increased significantly in Parkinson’s patients. Although hippuric acid and octadecanal was observed to be higher in PD patients, they did not increase significantly [ 2 ]. As eicosane increased the most significantly, we will use this as the biomarker related to seborrheic dermatitis.

LadA is a long-chain alkane monooxygenase capable of converting eicosane into its corresponding primary alcohol, 1-eicosanol [ 7 ]. We will use part BBa_K398017 from the iGEM registry as our LadA equivalent to show that eicosane can be converted into eicosanol. An alcohol dehydrogenase promoter is incorporated in the biosensor for the detection of eicosanol and RFP will be expressed. As the fluorescence is proportional to the level of eicosanol present, we would expect a higher fluorescence in PD patients compared to a control.

The Glutathione biosensor

Glutathione is an antioxidant that protects cells by preventing cellular damage and disruption of metabolic pathways. This molecule has a strong association with PD as the levels in the brain and blood decreases; the lower the levels of glutathione, the more severe Parkinson’s symptoms [ 7 ]. There are currently no methods of detecting glutathione as glutathione cannot be detected directly through a transcription factor. The team aims to detect changes in glutathione levels by measuring the induced fluorescence associated with hydrolysed glutathione product glycine, as well as transcription factors gcvR and gcvA. As PD patients have lower levels of glutathione in the blood than the average person, we would expect an optimised system to display higher levels of fluorescence in a control patient and lower fluorescence in a PD patient.

Our inspiration

“Super Smeller” Joy Milne had a unique ability to distinguish the “smell” of Parkinson’s on her late husband. Joy described her husband Les as smelling “musky” up-to 10 years prior to his Parkinson’s diagnosis [ 2 ]. In a pilot study conducted at Edinburgh University, a T-shirt sampling smell test of 12 individuals: 6 participants had diagnosed Parkinson’s disease and 6 were healthy controls. Milne correctly identified 11/12 subjects; with misidentification of one control subject. However, the misidentified control was diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease several months later, indicating Milne was correct on all counts [ 8 ]. The musky odour was later linked to seborrheic dermatitis, a common inflammatory skin disorder. The over-production of sebum characterised by Malassezia growth, this is a non-motor symptom of Parkinson’s due to immune deficiency [ 9 ].
